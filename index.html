4-Step LLM Learning Path

Starting point — the roadmap
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

Imagine you want to build a house. Would you start by putting up the roof? No way! You'd start with the foundation, then walls, then the roof, and finally the furniture inside. 🏗️

Learning AI works EXACTLY the same way. There are 4 steps, and you MUST do them in order:

Step 1 is learning the basics — what is a neural network? How does a machine learn from data? This is your foundation. Skip this, and everything else feels like magic you can't explain. 🧱

Step 2 is learning about Transformers — the special brain design from 2017 that changed everything. It's like learning how the engine works before you try to build a car. 🏎️

Step 3 is the fun part — pre-training (teaching the brain from scratch, costs $100 MILLION!), fine-tuning (teaching it YOUR specific job, much cheaper), and RAG (giving it a cheat sheet to look things up without retraining). 📚

Step 4 is building real things — AI agents that can use tools, browse the web, and actually DO stuff. This is the roof and furniture — it only works because you built everything underneath first! 🤖

The secret? All the resources for these 4 steps are FREE. Andrew Ng, Jay Alammar, Hugging Face — they all teach this for free online!

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

Here's the thing most people get wrong when learning AI — they jump straight to the flashy stuff. They want to build AI agents and chatbots on day one, without understanding how any of it works underneath. It's like trying to customize a car engine when you've never learned what a piston does. The 4-step learning path exists because each step is the foundation for the next, and skipping ahead means everything feels like unexplainable magic.

Step 1 is ML fundamentals — neural networks, gradient descent, how a machine actually 'learns' from data. This is the part most people skip because it feels academic. But without it, you can't debug anything. When your model isn't working, you won't know if the problem is in your data, your architecture, or your training loop. Andrew Ng's free courses cover this in about 2 weeks, and it pays dividends forever.

Step 2 is Transformers — the architecture that changed everything in 2017. Before Transformers, AI models read text one word at a time (RNNs) and forgot the beginning of a sentence by the time they reached the end. Transformers read everything at once using a mechanism called 'attention.' Jay Alammar's 'Illustrated Transformer' blog is considered the single best resource for understanding this — it turns dense math into visual intuition.

Step 3 is where the money is — literally. Pre-training means teaching a model from scratch on the entire internet, and it costs $10M to $100M+. Fine-tuning takes that pre-trained model and specializes it for your specific task, for a fraction of the cost. And RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) is the clever shortcut — instead of retraining the model, you give it a search engine to look things up in real time. Most production AI systems today use RAG because retraining is too expensive and too slow.

Step 4 is applications — AI agents that can browse the web, call APIs, write code, and actually DO things in the real world. This is where the industry is headed in 2025-2026, but it only works when you understand everything underneath. An agent that calls a tool is just a Transformer generating a special token that triggers a function call — if you didn't learn Steps 1-3, that sentence means nothing to you.

The most surprising part? Every resource for all 4 steps is free. Andrew Ng (Coursera), Jay Alammar (blog), Hugging Face (courses and models), and dozens of open-source projects. The barrier to becoming an AI engineer isn't money — it's discipline. You have to resist the temptation to skip ahead.

How LLMs Are Built + Data Prep

Topic 1 — zoom into Step 3 of the roadmap
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

Imagine you want to teach a robot to speak every language in the world. Where do you start? The INTERNET! 🌐

First, you send out a web crawler — a little spider bot that visits 2.7 BILLION web pages and copies everything it finds. That's 200-400 terabytes of raw messy text. Think of it like vacuuming the entire internet into a giant bag. 🕷️

But that bag is FILTHY — it's got HTML code, duplicate pages, people's private info, and toxic content. So you run it through a cleaning machine. Common Crawl collects the raw data, and teams like Hugging Face clean it into something called FineWeb — 44 terabytes of squeaky clean text. 🧹

Now you feed that clean text into the model and let it train. This is the EXPENSIVE part — GPT-3 cost around $4-5 million in compute, GPT-4 cost over $100 million! Thousands of GPUs running for months. 💸

But here's the funny part — after all that training, the model is like a genius who never learned manners. It knows EVERYTHING but can't hold a conversation! So you do post-training — teach it to be helpful and safe. That's much cheaper and faster. 🎓

And THAT'S how every LLM is built — vacuum the internet, clean it, train on it, then teach it manners!

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

Most people think building an LLM starts with some brilliant algorithm. It doesn't. It starts with a vacuum cleaner. Specifically, a web crawler that visits 2.7 billion web pages and downloads everything it finds — every Wikipedia article, every Reddit thread, every blog post, every news story. The result is a staggering 200 to 400 terabytes of raw text. An organization called Common Crawl has been doing this since 2008, and their dataset is freely available to anyone. This is the raw material that every major LLM is built from.

But here's what most people don't realize — that raw data is absolutely filthy. It's full of HTML tags, JavaScript code, duplicate pages (the same article copied across 50 websites), people's personal information (names, emails, phone numbers), and toxic content. You can't just feed this into a model. Data cleaning is roughly 90% of the work in building an LLM, and it's the least glamorous part. Teams like Hugging Face spent months building FineWeb — a cleaned version of Common Crawl that went from 200+ TB of mess down to 44 terabytes of high-quality text, roughly 15 trillion tokens. The cleaning pipeline includes deduplication, PII removal, language filtering, quality scoring, and toxic content filtering.

Once the data is clean, it gets tokenized — converted from human-readable text into numbers that the model can process. Then comes pre-training: you feed those trillions of tokens into the model architecture and let it learn patterns. This is where the enormous cost comes in. GPT-3 cost an estimated $4-5 million in compute alone. GPT-4 is estimated at over $100 million. You're running thousands of specialized GPUs (like NVIDIA's H100s) for weeks or months straight, burning through electricity and compute.

After pre-training, you have something remarkable but also useless for conversation — a 'base model.' It can finish any sentence brilliantly because it's seen the entire internet, but ask it 'What's the capital of France?' and instead of answering, it might generate ten more questions (because on the internet, questions are often followed by more questions, not answers). It's a genius who never learned manners.

That's why every LLM goes through post-training — first Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) to teach it the format of helpful conversations, then Reinforcement Learning (RL) to make it safe and honest. The entire pipeline from crawling to deployment can take 6-12 months and cost tens of millions of dollars. And yet, the resulting model — the thing that seems like magic when you chat with it — is just statistics learned from the internet.

Tokenization

Topic 2 — data is clean, now convert text → numbers
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

Here's a problem: computers don't understand words. They ONLY understand numbers. So how do you get a computer to read "Tell me a joke"? You need a translator! That translator is called a Tokenizer. 🔢

The tokenizer has a dictionary — a vocabulary — with every word-piece it knows and a number for each one. "Tell" = 41, "me" = 553, "a" = 264, "joke" = 22691. Now the computer can work with it!

But how big should the dictionary be? If you use WHOLE WORDS, your dictionary needs 500,000+ entries (every word on the internet!). That's way too big. If you use SINGLE LETTERS, the dictionary is tiny (just 26 letters!) but "Hello" becomes 5 tokens instead of 1 — everything takes forever. ❌

The sweet spot? Sub-word tokenization! Break words into PIECES. "unhappiness" becomes "un" + "happiness". Common words stay whole, rare words get split up. Dictionary size: 50,000 to 200,000 entries. Perfect balance! ✅

The most popular way to build this dictionary is called BPE — Byte Pair Encoding. It starts with individual letters, then keeps merging the most popular pairs together. "h"+"u"+"g" → "hu"+"g" → "hug". Like building bigger LEGO pieces from smaller ones until you have the right set! 🧱

Fun fact: type gibberish like "xqzplm" and it explodes into tiny tokens. Type "the cat sat" and it's just 3 tokens. The tokenizer knows common words!

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

Here's a fundamental problem that most people never think about — computers don't understand text. Not even a little bit. They only understand numbers. So before any AI model can process the sentence 'Tell me a joke,' that sentence needs to be converted into a sequence of numbers. That's what a tokenizer does, and the way it does it matters more than you'd expect.

The obvious approach is word-level tokenization — give every word its own number. 'Tell' = 41, 'me' = 553, 'joke' = 22691. Simple, right? The problem is vocabulary size. The English internet contains over 500,000 unique words (including misspellings, slang, technical terms, names). Your vocabulary table becomes enormous, and every new word the model encounters is 'unknown.' The opposite extreme — character-level tokenization — uses a tiny vocabulary (just 26 letters plus punctuation), but now 'Hello' is 5 tokens instead of 1. Sequences become ~5x longer, which means ~5x more computation, ~5x more memory, and the model struggles to learn word-level meaning from individual characters.

The breakthrough was sub-word tokenization — specifically an algorithm called BPE (Byte Pair Encoding). BPE starts with individual characters and repeatedly merges the most frequent pair into a new token. First pass: 'h' and 'e' appear together constantly, so merge them into 'he.' Next pass: 'he' and 'l' merge into 'hel.' Keep going until you reach your target vocabulary size — typically 50,000 to 200,000 tokens. The result is elegant: common words like 'the' stay as single tokens, while rare words like 'unhappiness' get split into 'un' + 'happiness.' The model gets the efficiency of word-level for common text and the flexibility of character-level for rare words.

One thing that surprises people is that every model has its OWN tokenizer with its OWN vocabulary. GPT-4 uses roughly 100,000 tokens (cl100k_base). GPT-4o expanded to about 200,000 tokens (o200k_base). The same sentence produces completely different token IDs across different models — 'Hello' might be token 15339 in GPT-3 and token 9906 in Llama. This is why you can't just swap models in a pipeline without also swapping the tokenizer.

You can actually see this in action — type a common phrase like 'the cat sat' and it's just 3 tokens. Type gibberish like 'xqzplm' and it explodes into 5-6 tiny tokens because the tokenizer has never seen that character combination before. The tokenizer is the unsung hero of the entire LLM pipeline — invisible when it works, catastrophic when it doesn't.

Transformers

Topic 3 — tokens exist, now what brain design processes them?
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

In 2017, a team at Google published a paper called "Attention Is All You Need" and changed EVERYTHING. Before this, AI read text one word at a time, like reading through a tiny keyhole — slow and forgetful. 🔑

The Transformer reads ALL words at once! And it has a superpower called "attention" — it figures out which words are important to each other. Read this: "The cat sat on the mat because IT was tired." What does "it" mean? The cat or the mat? YOU know it's the cat. The Transformer figures this out too — by letting every word "look at" every other word and decide what matters. That's attention! 👁️

The original Transformer had two halves: an Encoder (reads and understands text) and a Decoder (generates new text). It was built for translation — French in, English out. 🇫🇷➡️🇬🇧

But then researchers made a wild discovery: throw away the encoder, keep ONLY the decoder, and you get something that's AMAZING at generating text. One word at a time, predicting what comes next. This is called a "decoder-only" transformer. 🔮

And here's the punchline — ChatGPT, Claude, Llama, Gemini — EVERY modern AI chatbot uses this exact same decoder-only design. The 2017 paper literally changed the world! 🌍

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

Before 2017, AI language models had a serious problem — they read text one word at a time, in order, like looking through a keyhole. These were called RNNs (Recurrent Neural Networks), and they had a fatal flaw: by the time they reached word 50 in a sentence, they'd largely forgotten word 1. Imagine reading a novel but only remembering the last paragraph. Long documents, complex references, subtle context — all lost. Researchers tried patches (LSTMs, GRUs), but the fundamental bottleneck remained: sequential processing meant you couldn't parallelize training, and the 'memory' degraded over distance.

In 2017, a team at Google published a paper titled 'Attention Is All You Need' — arguably the most important AI paper ever written. Their key insight was radical: throw away the sequential processing entirely. Instead, let every word in a sentence look at every OTHER word simultaneously. This mechanism is called 'self-attention.' Consider the sentence: 'The cat sat on the mat because IT was tired.' What does 'it' refer to? The cat or the mat? Humans resolve this instantly. Self-attention does the same thing — 'it' computes a relevance score with every other word, discovers that 'cat' has the highest score, and strengthens that connection. This happens for ALL words at ALL positions at the same time. That's the magic.

The original Transformer architecture had two halves — an Encoder (which reads and understands the input) and a Decoder (which generates the output). It was designed for machine translation: feed French into the encoder, get English out of the decoder. But researchers quickly discovered something unexpected. If you throw away the encoder and keep only the decoder, you get a model that's extraordinarily good at generating text — one token at a time, predicting what comes next based on everything before it. This 'decoder-only' architecture became the foundation of a revolution.

GPT-1 (2018) proved the concept with 117 million parameters. GPT-2 (2019) scaled to 1.5 billion. GPT-3 (2020) exploded to 175 billion — and suddenly, the model could write essays, code, and poetry. The architecture hadn't changed. The math was identical. They just made it bigger and fed it more data. That's the shocking simplicity of the Transformer — it's the same building block, stacked and scaled.

Today, every major AI model you interact with — ChatGPT, Claude, Llama, Gemini, Mistral — is a decoder-only Transformer. The 2017 paper didn't just introduce a new technique; it obsoleted an entire generation of AI architectures. Jay Alammar's 'Illustrated Transformer' blog post remains the best visual explanation of how attention works under the hood, and it's free to read.

Model Architecture

Topics 3 + 4 — combine tokens + transformers into the full pipeline
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

Imagine you're playing a word-guessing game with a really smart robot. You say "I hope you are" and the robot has to guess what comes next. 🤖

But here's the thing — the robot can't read words! It only understands numbers. So first, a helper called the Tokenizer chops your words into secret number codes. "I" becomes 42, "hope" becomes 891, and so on.

But plain numbers aren't enough. The robot needs RICHER numbers — like a whole list of clues for each word. So the Embedding Layer turns each number code into a long list of clues. Think of it like turning a name tag into a full personality profile! 🏷️➡️📋

Now comes the big brain part. Those clue-lists pass through a bunch of Transformer Blocks — like a stack of really smart filters. Each filter makes the clues better and better. GPT-2 Small has 12 filters (117M params). The full GPT-2 has 48. GPT-3 has 96. Same type of filter, just MORE of them — like building a taller LEGO tower! 🧱

Finally, the robot looks at the LAST clue-list and asks: "Out of all 50,000 words I know, which one should come next?" It gives every word a score. "well" gets 86 points. "happy" gets 11. "banana" gets 0. Highest score wins! 🏆

And that's it — that's how ChatGPT, Claude, and every AI chatbot works. Same recipe, just some use bigger LEGO towers than others!

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

If you open the hood of any modern AI chatbot — ChatGPT, Claude, Llama, Gemini — you'll find the exact same 4-stage pipeline. Not similar. Identical in structure. The only difference between a small model and a massive one is the size of each stage. Understanding this pipeline is like understanding the assembly line in a factory — once you see it, every model makes sense.

Stage 1 is the Tokenizer, which chops text into token IDs (covered in the tokenization topic). Stage 2 is where it gets interesting — the Embedding Layer. This takes each token ID (just a plain number like 42) and converts it into a dense vector — a list of hundreds or thousands of decimal numbers. Think of it as turning a name tag into a full personality profile. Token 42 ('I') becomes something like [0.2, -0.8, 0.5, 1.1, ...] — a 768-dimensional vector in GPT-2, or a 12,288-dimensional vector in GPT-3. These embedding vectors aren't hardcoded; they're learned during training. The model discovers that similar words should have similar vectors — 'king' and 'queen' end up near each other in this high-dimensional space.

Stage 3 is the Transformer Blocks — the actual 'brain' of the model. Each block contains the self-attention mechanism (from the Transformer paper) plus a feed-forward neural network. GPT-2 XL (1.5B) has 48 blocks stacked on top of each other. GPT-3 has 96. Llama 3 has 126. Same type of block, just more of them — like building a taller LEGO tower. Every block refines the vectors further, adding more context and understanding. By the time the vectors exit the final block, they've been enriched with deep contextual meaning.

Stage 4 is the head — Linear projection followed by Softmax. Here's the critical detail most people miss: only the LAST token's vector matters. The model takes that single vector, projects it across the entire vocabulary (roughly 50,000 words), and Softmax converts those raw scores into probabilities that sum to 1.0. 'Well' might get 0.86 (86%), 'happy' gets 0.11, 'banana' gets 0.0001. The highest probability wins — or more precisely, a sampling algorithm picks from the top candidates.

What makes this truly elegant is that GPT-2 (1.5 billion parameters), GPT-3 (175 billion), and Llama 3 (405 billion) all use this EXACT same 4-stage pipeline. The only things that change are the hyperparameters — number of layers, vector dimension, vocabulary size. The architecture is identical. It's like the difference between a 2-story house and a 96-story skyscraper — same blueprint, just scaled up.

Model Training

Topic 5 — architecture is set, now how does it LEARN?
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

Imagine you build a robot that finishes your sentences. You say "peanut butter and..." and it should say "jelly!" But when you first turn it on, it says "elephant!" because its brain is full of random nonsense. 🤖

So here's how you teach it. You grab a sentence from a book — "The cat sat on the..." — and you KNOW the answer is "mat." You let the robot guess. It says "pizza!" with 15% confidence. Then you use a GRADE MACHINE (that's the loss function) — it compares "pizza" with "mat" and gives a grade: 3.0. In this game, high is BAD — like golf! ⛳

Then a HELPER ROBOT (that's the optimizer) looks at the grade, goes into the robot's brain, and turns billions of tiny knobs so that "mat" gets a little more likely and "pizza" gets a little less likely next time. 🔧

One round doesn't fix it. But you do this a MILLION times with different sentences. Slowly, the robot stops saying "elephant" and starts saying "jelly." Not because anyone TOLD it peanut butter goes with jelly — it figured it out from seeing the pattern thousands of times! That's implicit knowledge. 🧠

The code for this? About 10 lines. But running it on 405 billion knobs? That needs 16,000 of the world's best computers running for weeks, costing millions of dollars. Simple recipe, enormous kitchen! 🏗️

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

Here's what surprises most people about training — the algorithm is shockingly simple. It's a 2-step loop. Step 1 (the forward pass): feed a sentence into the model with the last word hidden, let the model guess what that word should be, and compute a single number called the 'loss' using cross-entropy. Loss is like a golf score — high is bad. If the correct answer is 'physicist' and the model guesses it with only 5% confidence, the loss is high. If the model guesses it with 90% confidence, the loss is nearly zero.

Step 2 (the backward pass): the optimizer takes that loss number and works backward through every single layer of the model, computing exactly how much each weight contributed to the error. Then it nudges each weight by a tiny amount in the direction that would reduce the loss. This is called gradient descent — literally descending the slope of the error landscape. The model has billions of these weights (405 billion in Llama 3), and every single one gets adjusted, every single round.

Repeat this loop a million times with different sentences from the internet, and something remarkable happens. The model starts discovering patterns that nobody explicitly taught it. It learns that 'Einstein' connects to 'physicist,' that 'machine learning' connects to 'algorithms,' that 'peanut butter' connects to 'jelly' — not because anyone programmed these associations, but because the statistical patterns in the training data made these connections reduce the loss. This is called 'implicit knowledge,' and it's why LLMs can seem to 'understand' things — they don't understand in the human sense, they've internalized statistical relationships across the entire internet.

The code for this training loop is about 10 lines of Python. The math is undergraduate calculus. There's nothing exotic about it. What IS exotic is the scale. Training Llama 3 (405 billion parameters) required 16,000 NVIDIA H100 GPUs, over 7 terabytes of GPU memory, and cost millions of dollars in compute. Meta's training cluster consumed enough electricity to power a small town. The training ran for weeks, processing trillions of tokens.

One critical detail that trips people up: training happens ONCE. It's not an ongoing process. You gather your data, you run the training loop for weeks, and then you stop. The model's weights are frozen. When you chat with ChatGPT, it's not learning from your conversation — it's running inference on frozen weights. The next version (GPT-5, Claude 4) will be a completely separate training run from scratch, with new data and new compute. Training is a one-time investment that produces a static artifact.

Text Generation

Topic 6 — model is trained, now how does it PRODUCE text?
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

Imagine you have a super smart robot that knows every word in every book ever written. You ask it to finish your sentence: "Once upon a..." But the robot doesn't just KNOW the answer — it has a list of 50,000 possible next words, each with a score of how likely it is. 🤖

So how does it pick? The lazy way (Greedy Search) is to always pick the word with the highest score. "time" wins at 40%! Easy! But here's the problem — the robot gets stuck in a loop. Like a kid who tells the same joke over and over: "and then... and then... and then..." It picks the same popular words FOREVER. 🔄

So smart scientists said: "What if we let the robot be a LITTLE random?" That's Top-P Sampling. Instead of picking just the #1 word, you grab all the top words until their scores add up to 90%. If the robot is SURE the next word is "time" (89% confident), it only looks at that one word. But if it's unsure — maybe "time" is 30%, "a" is 25%, "the" is 20% — it grabs ALL of those and randomly picks from them. The robot adapts! Confident = focused. Unsure = explore! 🎯

And there's a temperature knob. Turn it DOWN for homework (be precise!). Turn it UP for storytelling (be wild!). That's why when you ask ChatGPT to write code, it's careful and exact. But when you ask it to write a poem, it surprises you every time. 🌡️

The whole thing is just a for loop: pick a word, add it to the sentence, pick the next word, repeat. Stop when the robot says "THE END" or you hit a word limit. That's it — that's how every AI writes! ✨

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

Here's something most people don't realize — after all that expensive training ($100M+, months of GPU time), the model doesn't actually 'know' what to say. It knows probabilities. You type 'Albert' and the model runs a forward pass through its billions of parameters and outputs a probability distribution over its entire vocabulary — roughly 50,000 possible next tokens. 'Einstein' might get 42%, 'John' gets 15%, 'was' gets 8%, and so on. The question is: how do you pick from that list?

The naive approach is Greedy Search — always pick the highest probability token. Simple, fast, but fatally flawed. It causes repetitive loops because the model keeps choosing the same 'safe' high-probability words. OpenAI demonstrated this with GPT-2: ask it about a cute dog and it literally gets stuck repeating the same sentence. Beam Search improved on this by tracking K parallel paths (like exploring K branches of a decision tree simultaneously), but it's still deterministic — same input always produces the same output, which makes the text feel robotic.

The real breakthrough was moving from deterministic to stochastic (random) sampling. Top-K Sampling picks randomly from the K most likely tokens — but K is fixed, which is a problem. Sometimes the model is very confident (one token has 90% probability) and you want to focus. Other times it's unsure and you want to explore. Top-P (Nucleus) Sampling solved this elegantly: instead of a fixed K, you keep adding tokens from highest to lowest probability until their cumulative probability exceeds a threshold P (usually 0.9). This means K is DYNAMIC — when confident, only 1-2 tokens qualify. When uncertain, maybe 20 tokens qualify. The model naturally adapts its creativity based on context.

Finally, Temperature acts as a creativity dial on TOP of the sampling algorithm. Low temperature (0.1-0.3) sharpens the probability distribution — the rich get richer, making output precise and focused (ideal for code generation). High temperature (0.7-1.0) flattens it — giving underdog tokens a fighting chance, making output more creative and surprising (ideal for storytelling, poetry). Every time you adjust that 'temperature' slider in ChatGPT or Claude, this is exactly what's happening.

The entire generation process is surprisingly simple in code — it's a for loop. Feed in the prompt, get probabilities, sample one token, append it to the input, repeat. Stop when the model outputs a special <EOS> (End of Sequence) token, or when you hit a maximum length. That's it. Every AI response you've ever read was generated exactly this way — one token at a time, in a loop.

Post Training SFT

Topic 7 — model generates text, now make it actually useful
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

Remember that genius who never learned manners? The one who read the entire internet but can't hold a conversation? That's a base model after pre-training. It can finish any sentence, but ask it "What's the capital of France?" and it might just keep rambling instead of answering. 🤷

SFT — Supervised Fine-Tuning — is like sending that genius to finishing school. You give it 14,000 to 100,000 examples of perfect conversations: "Question: What's 2+2? Answer: 4." Over and over, until it learns the FORMAT of being helpful. 📝

Here's the wild part — the training algorithm is IDENTICAL to pre-training! Same math, same optimizer, same loop. The ONLY thing that changes is the DATA. Instead of messy internet text, you feed it carefully curated question-answer pairs written by experts. Quality over quantity! ✨

After SFT, the model can answer questions. But it's still not safe — it might help you do dangerous things if you ask nicely. That's where step 2 comes in: Reinforcement Learning (RL) teaches it to be safe and honest. 🛡️

Think of it like Pega: Pre-training = installing the platform. SFT = configuring it for your business. RL = user testing to catch edge cases. Same platform, just refined step by step!

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

After pre-training, you have a model that's absorbed the entire internet — but it's essentially useless as an assistant. Ask a base model 'What's the capital of France?' and instead of answering 'Paris,' it might generate 'What's the capital of Germany? What's the capital of Spain?' — because on the internet, lists of questions are common, so generating more questions is a statistically reasonable next-token prediction. The base model is a text completion engine, not a conversation partner. It has all the knowledge but none of the behavior.

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) fixes this by showing the model thousands of examples of the exact behavior you want. You create a dataset of instruction-response pairs: 'Summarize this article' → [perfect summary]. 'Write a Python function that...' → [clean code]. 'Explain quantum physics simply' → [clear explanation]. InstructGPT used roughly 13,000 of these pairs. Stanford's Alpaca used 52,000. Dolly used 15,000. The surprising discovery was that you don't need millions — a few thousand high-quality examples are enough to fundamentally shift the model's behavior.

Here's the part that blows people's minds — the training algorithm for SFT is IDENTICAL to pre-training. Same forward pass, same cross-entropy loss function, same optimizer, same backward pass. Nothing changes in the math. The ONLY difference is the data format. Pre-training uses raw internet text (trillions of tokens, months of compute, $100M+). SFT uses curated Q&A pairs (thousands of examples, hours of compute, much cheaper). Same algorithm, radically different data, completely different behavior. This is one of the most elegant insights in modern AI — you don't need a new algorithm to teach new behavior, you just need the right examples.

But SFT alone isn't enough. An SFT-trained model will follow instructions, but it has no concept of safety. Ask it to write malware, and it'll try its best — because the SFT data taught it to be helpful, not to be cautious. That's where Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) comes in. Human raters rank multiple model responses from best to worst, a 'reward model' learns those preferences, and then the LLM is fine-tuned to maximize the reward signal. This teaches the model to refuse harmful requests, avoid hallucination, and be honest about uncertainty.

The full post-training pipeline is: Base Model → SFT (learns to follow instructions) → RLHF (learns to be safe and honest) → Production Model. Think of it in Pega terms: pre-training is installing the platform, SFT is configuring it for your business process, and RLHF is UAT testing to catch edge cases. Same platform at every stage — just progressively refined with better data and feedback.

Reinforcement Learning

Topic 8 — SFT model answers questions, now make it GOOD at answering
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

Imagine you taught a robot to answer homework questions (that was SFT). It answers, but it's lazy — ask "how do I learn math?" and it says "read a book." Technically correct, but not helpful! 😤

So you give the robot a practice drill. You ask it the same question 7 times and it gives 7 different answers. Some are great ("start with addition, then multiplication, then practice with fun games!") and some are terrible ("just give up" 😅). You mark the good ones with ✅ and bad ones with ❌, then tell the robot: "Make more answers like the green ones!"

But here's the tricky part — for math homework, you can just CHECK if 2+2=4. Easy! ✅ That's a verifiable task. But what about "write me a poem"? There's no right answer! 🤔 So you hire a bunch of teachers to RANK the poems: "this one's best, this one's okay, this one stinks." Then you build a robot teacher (the reward model) that learns from those rankings. Now the robot teacher can score poems WITHOUT needing humans every time!

The secret sauce? An algorithm called PPO that takes all those scores and adjusts the robot's brain so it writes better answers next time. Practice → score → adjust → repeat. That's reinforcement learning — turning a lazy C-student robot into an A-student! 🏆

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

Here's something most people don't realize: after supervised fine-tuning, your LLM can answer questions — but it gives you the equivalent of a one-sentence text message when you asked for directions. "Take Andrew Ng's course." Thanks, but not helpful. Reinforcement learning exists because correctness isn't enough — you need quality.

The mechanism is elegant: it's a practice algorithm. You feed a prompt to the SFT model and let it generate seven different responses. Some are detailed and helpful, others are lazy or even harmful ("shame on you, try meditation" — good advice, terrible delivery). The trick is scoring them: which responses should the model produce MORE of?

This is where the world splits into two categories. Verifiable tasks — math, coding — have checkable answers. Is 2+2=4? Run the code, does it pass? A Python script labels each response correct or incorrect, and an algorithm called PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) — or the newer GRPO — updates the model parameters to reinforce correct answers. Simple, automatic, done.

Unverifiable tasks — writing, brainstorming, naming your startup — have no right answer. So you need RLHF: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, a two-step process. First, hire human annotators to rank responses: "Paris" beats "It's in Europe" beats "Eiffel Tower." Convert those rankings into winning/losing pairs. Train a separate reward model using margin ranking loss — a loss function that pushes the winning response's score UP and the losing response's score DOWN, maximizing the gap. Now this reward model is your proxy for humans — it scores any response automatically. Step two: same PPO loop. Model practices, reward model scores, PPO reinforces high-scoring responses.

The proof is in the demo: Llama 3.1 405B Base just rambles with more questions when asked "how to learn ML?" The post-trained version delivers a formatted, step-by-step guide with bold headers. Four stages — pre-training (trillions of tokens, months), SFT (10K-100K pairs, days), reward model (human-ranked comparisons, margin ranking loss), and RL (PPO reinforcement, days) — transform a trillion-parameter text completer into ChatGPT.

Evaluation of LLMs

Topic 9 — model is trained, now how do we know if it's any good?
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

Imagine your school is having a bake-off and you need to judge whose cookies are best! 🍪

First, you try judging in your kitchen BEFORE the bake sale — that's offline evaluation. You have three ways: You could count ingredients exactly (perplexity — "did they follow the recipe perfectly?") but that's boring because perfect-recipe cookies can still taste terrible! 😝 So instead you give them REAL tests — "make chocolate chip cookies, make brownies, make a cake" — and see how each one turns out. That's benchmarks like PIQA and HellaSwag. Or you could hire a fancy chef to taste everything, but chefs are biased — one loves chocolate, another hates it! 🧑‍🍳

Then comes the bake sale — cookies are OUT and real people are eating them. That's online evaluation! People give thumbs up 👍 or thumbs down 👎 while eating (that's human feedback in ChatGPT). But the COOLEST way? LM Arena — like a BLIND taste test! 🎭 Someone puts TWO cookies in front of you, you don't know who baked them, you pick your favorite, and THEN they reveal who made it! UC Berkeley researchers built this for AI, and 2.9 MILLION people have voted!

Oh, and the bakery itself? There's a GUARD at the door checking if your ORDER is okay 🚪 (no "make me poison cookies!"), then a HELPER who fixes your messy handwriting on the order 📝, then the BAKER makes the cookies 🍪, and ANOTHER guard checks the cookies BEFORE you eat them! Two guards — one checks what goes IN, one checks what comes OUT! 🛡️🛡️

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

Here's something most people get wrong about LLM evaluation: they think it's one thing. It's actually two completely different worlds — and confusing them is like confusing a lab test with a customer review.

Offline evaluation happens before anyone sees the model. The oldest method is perplexity — feed the model "How are you" and measure how confidently it predicts "doing" next (probability 0.93). Lower perplexity means better prediction. But here's why nobody trusts it anymore: a model that perfectly reproduces text can still give useless answers to real questions. Reproducing isn't the same as reasoning. So the industry moved to task-specific benchmarks — standardized tests across six domains: common-sense reasoning (PIQA, HellaSwag), world knowledge, reading comprehension, math (GSM8K), code generation (HumanEval), and composite benchmarks (MMLU). You run thousands of questions through the model, compare answers against the correct ones, and score it. You can also hire human experts, but they're expensive, slow, and biased — two experts will score the same response differently.

Online evaluation happens after deployment with real users. The simple version: ChatGPT's thumbs up/thumbs down button. Every click is a signal — used for evaluation AND fed back into RL training (dual purpose). But the gold standard is LM Arena (lmarena.ai), built by UC Berkeley researchers (the LMSYS group). It's a blind taste test for AI: you submit a prompt, two anonymous models respond, you vote for the better one, identities are revealed after. With 2.9 million votes across 211+ models, it produces ELO rankings like chess. Gemini 2.5 Pro leads at 1446, followed by o3 at 1435.

And evaluation is just one layer. The full system design reveals that the trained LLM is a tiny piece of the puzzle. Every prompt passes through an input safety filter (guardrails), a prompt enhancer (fixes typos, grammar, ambiguity using ML + heuristics), the response generator (Top-p sampling with the LLM), and then an output safety evaluator — a second set of guardrails checking the response before the user sees it. Two checkpoints, not one. Session management appends your full chat history to every new prompt — that's how ChatGPT "remembers" your conversation.

The punchline? Building the LLM takes months and millions of dollars. But the system around it — the guards, the enhancers, the memory — is what turns a statistical model into a product 2.9 million people vote on.

LLM Adaptation & Fine-Tuning

Week 2 begins — from general-purpose to specialized LLMs
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

Imagine you have a super smart friend who read every book in the library. You can ask them math questions, writing help, or coding problems and they nail it every time! 🧒

But then you open a retail store and ask your friend: "What's our refund policy?" They look confused and just make something up! 😱 They never read YOUR store's rule book — they only know general stuff from the library.

So how do you teach your friend about YOUR store? There are 3 ways:

Way 1: FINE-TUNING — You sit your friend down and make them study your entire rule book cover to cover. Now they KNOW your policies by heart! 📖 But there's a catch — if your friend has a HUGE brain (billions of brain cells), updating ALL of them takes forever and costs a fortune! 💸

So smart people invented a shortcut: instead of retraining your friend's ENTIRE brain, you just stick some tiny Post-it notes on their brain! 📝 These Post-it notes (called Adapters or LoRA) are tiny — only 0.1% of the brain — but they're enough to teach the new stuff. The original brain stays frozen, and only the Post-it notes learn!

LoRA is especially cool — it takes each brain connection and adds a tiny side-road next to it. The main highway stays the same, but the little side-road learns the new stuff. Only 3 MILLION Post-it notes instead of 3 BILLION brain cells! That's 1,000x cheaper! 🎯

Way 2: PROMPT ENGINEERING — Just give really good instructions each time (coming next!) Way 3: RAG — Give them a cheat sheet to look stuff up (coming soon!) 📚

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

Here's something that trips up most beginners: they assume ChatGPT can answer anything. It can't. Ask it about your company's refund policy, your internal docs, or your proprietary data — and it will confidently hallucinate an answer. It has never seen your data. General-purpose LLMs are trained on the public internet, not your internal knowledge base. This is the adaptation problem, and Week 2 is all about solving it.

There are three techniques to adapt an LLM: fine-tuning, prompt engineering, and RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation). Fine-tuning is the most obvious — you continue training the model on your own documents. Feed it your PDFs, your knowledge base, your internal wikis. After training, the model's weights physically encode your domain knowledge. Ask "What's the refund policy?" and it answers from its learned weights, not from hallucination. But here's the problem: modern LLMs have billions of parameters. Updating all of them requires enormous compute — thousands of GPU hours. For a 3-billion parameter model, that means adjusting 3 billion numbers.

This is where Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) changes the game. Instead of updating all 3 billion parameters, you freeze the original model and inject tiny trainable layers. Adapters (2019) were first — freeze the original weights (shown in gray), inject small trainable layers between existing ones (shown in red), and train only those. The original model stays untouched.

Then came LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation), which is even cleverer. For every linear layer in the model, LoRA adds two small matrices (A and B) as a side branch. The original weight matrix W stays frozen. Input X flows through both branches: the original path computes WX, the LoRA path computes ABX, and they add together. The key insight: these LoRA matrices are tiny. For a Qwen 2.5 3B model, LoRA adds only 3 million trainable parameters — that's 0.1% of the original 3 billion. You get 99.9% of the model for free and only train the last 0.1%. After training, you save just the LoRA weights, load them at inference, and your specialized model is ready.

The punchline? Fine-tuning used to mean retraining everything — weeks of compute, millions of dollars. LoRA made it something you can do on a single GPU in hours. That democratization is why every startup can now build specialized AI products.

Prompt Engineering

Topic 11 — prompt engineering is one of the 3 adaptation methods
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

Imagine you have a super smart robot friend who read every book ever written, but when you ask "What's 2+2?", it just starts rambling about random stuff from books. It KNOWS the answer — it just doesn't know you want a SHORT DIRECT answer! 🤖

So you learn tricks to talk to it better. Trick 1 (Few-Shot): You show it examples first. "What's 1+1? 2. What's 3+3? 6. Now what's 2+2?" And the robot goes "4!" It copied the pattern! 🎯

Trick 2 (Zero-Shot): You don't even need examples — just say "Question: What's 2+2? Answer:" and the robot knows to answer directly. Just those format words were enough! ✨

Trick 3 (Chain of Thought): For harder problems, you show the robot HOW to think. Instead of just "the answer is 11", you write "5 balls + 2 cans of 3 = 6 balls, 5+6=11." Now when it gets a hard problem, it breaks it down step by step instead of guessing. A Google team found this one trick made their robot go from getting 18 out of 100 math problems right to 57 out of 100. Same robot, just better instructions! 🧠

Trick 4 (Role): Tell the robot "pretend you're a doctor" and suddenly its health answers get WAY better. It's playing a character! 🎭

But here's the catch — what if you want the robot to know everything about YOUR store? You'd have to stuff ALL your documents into the question. And there's a limit on how long your question can be — like a text message with a character limit. That's the problem, and that's what RAG fixes next! 📦

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

Here's something most people don't realize: you can dramatically change an LLM's behavior without touching a single weight. No training, no GPUs, no datasets — just better words in your prompt. That's prompt engineering, and one simple trick tripled a model's math accuracy overnight.

The landscape breaks into four techniques. Few-shot prompting means including example Q&A pairs in your prompt so the model pattern-matches your desired format. Show it three questions with direct answers, and it'll answer your fourth question the same way. The critical nuance: the quality of your examples determines the quality of your output — lazy three-word examples produce lazy three-word answers. Zero-shot prompting skips examples entirely and uses instructions or format cues — even placing "Q:" and "A:" labels is enough to flip a base model from rambling into answering.

But the real game-changer is Chain of Thought (CoT). A Google Brain paper from January 2022 by Wei et al. showed that including reasoning traces in your few-shot examples — not just "the answer is 11" but "5 + (2×3) = 5+6 = 11" — made a 540-billion-parameter model jump from 18% to 57% accuracy on math benchmarks. Same model, same parameters, zero retraining. Then, just 19 days later, another Google paper dropped the bombshell: you don't even need examples. Just append "Let's think step by step" to any prompt. Five words. The model starts decomposing problems into sequential reasoning steps, and accuracy soars. This insight later became the foundation for training "thinking models" like o1 and DeepSeek-R1.

In production, all these techniques live in the system prompt — hidden instructions the end user never sees. The user's question (user prompt) gets combined with the system prompt and sent to the LLM together. Every chatbot — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — works this way. For a retail store, you'd stuff your refund policy, product catalog, and FAQs into the system prompt so the LLM can answer customer questions from your actual docs instead of hallucinating.

But here's the wall: every LLM has a context window — a maximum number of tokens it can process. A few PDFs fit fine. A thousand-page knowledge base? Exceeds the limit, or becomes prohibitively expensive to process. This is exactly the problem that RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) solves — instead of stuffing everything in, you search for only the relevant documents and include just those. That's next.

RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation

Topic 12 — RAG solves the context window limitation of prompt engineering
📖 Explain It Like I'm 10

Remember how your smart robot friend has a text message limit? You wanted to paste ALL 10,000 of your company's PDFs into the message, but it's way too long! 📱❌

So you build a SMART SEARCH ENGINE for your robot! Here's how:

First, you hire a helper to READ all 10,000 PDFs. But PDFs are messy — they have pictures, tables, headers, all jumbled up. So the helper uses special tools (like LayoutParser) to figure out where the text is, where the pictures are, and extract everything neatly. That's called Document Parsing! 📄➡️📝

But some of those PDFs are 500 pages long! Way too big. So the helper CHOPS each document into small pieces — like cutting a pizza into slices. Each slice is called a "chunk." You can even make the slices overlap a little so you don't lose anything at the edges! That's Document Chunking! 🍕✂️

Now the MAGIC part. You give each chunk a special fingerprint — not a word fingerprint, but a MEANING fingerprint! You use a special model (called an embedding model) that reads each chunk and creates a list of numbers that captures what it MEANS. Chunks about refund policies get similar fingerprints. Chunks about shipping get different fingerprints. This comes from Word2Vec — the first model that showed "King minus Man plus Woman equals Queen" in number-land! 🔮

Now when someone asks "How do I get my money back?" — you turn THAT question into a fingerprint too, find the closest matching chunks, and ONLY send those to your robot. 10,000 PDFs → 3-5 relevant chunks. Problem solved! 🎯

Oh, and what about PICTURES in the PDFs? Two options: either use CLIP (a special model that understands both words AND images in the same fingerprint space) or describe the picture in words first, then fingerprint the description! 🖼️

🎬 60-Second Technical Version

Here's the fundamental problem with prompt engineering: context windows have a ceiling. You can stuff a few PDFs into the system prompt and the LLM will answer beautifully. But real companies have thousands — sometimes millions — of documents. No context window on earth can hold all of that. RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) solves this by adding a retrieval layer that searches your documents and passes only the relevant pieces to the LLM.

The architecture is elegant: two components, retrieval and generation. Generation is just the LLM. The interesting part is retrieval, and building it requires a three-step preprocessing pipeline. Step one is document parsing — converting raw PDFs and HTMLs into structured text. This involves layout detection (finding where titles, text blocks, figures, and tables are on a page) followed by text extraction using AI/OCR methods. Libraries like Docling and LayoutParser handle this in a few lines of code and work on everything from modern PDFs to scanned historical documents.

Step two is document chunking — breaking those long extracted texts into smaller, manageable pieces. A 500-page manual shouldn't be one giant chunk; it should be hundreds of focused pieces. Length-based chunking just counts characters (can break mid-sentence — bad). Regex-based splits on punctuation (better, but no semantic awareness). The best approach uses specialized splitters like LangChain's TextSplitters that understand document structure — they split at headers, sections, and code blocks. Two key hyperparameters: chunk_size (how big each piece should be) and chunk_overlap (how much adjacent chunks share at their edges, preventing important context from being lost at boundaries).

Step three is the heart of modern RAG: vector-based indexing. Traditional keyword search fails because "how do I get my money back" won't match a document about "refund policy" — same meaning, zero word overlap. Embedding models solve this by mapping each chunk into a high-dimensional vector where similar meanings land nearby. The concept traces back to the Word2Vec paper, which first demonstrated that vector arithmetic captures meaning: King - Man + Woman ≈ Queen. Today's models like OpenAI's text-embedding-3-large produce 3,072-dimensional vectors. You embed every chunk and store the results in an index table.

Images need special handling since PDFs often contain diagrams and figures. Option one: use CLIP (OpenAI's contrastive model) which has a shared text-image embedding space — text and matching images land near each other in the same vector space. Critical rule: you must use the same CLIP model's text encoder for your text chunks and its image encoder for images. Option two: run images through a captioning model first, converting them to text descriptions, then embed those with any text embedding model. Simpler pipeline, same result. Either way, you end up with two index tables — text index and image index — ready for runtime search. That search step, plus the generation component, is the next lecture.

Key Concepts Summary
1. **Sequential learning matters** - The 4 steps must be done in order; skipping foundations leads to shallow understanding
2. **ML/DL fundamentals come first** - Neural networks, gradient descent, backpropagation before anything else
3. **Transformers are THE architecture** - Every modern LLM (GPT, Claude, Llama) is built on transformers and attention
4. **Pre-training ≠ Fine-tuning ≠ RAG** - Three distinct techniques with different costs, scales, and use cases
5. **Agents are the final destination** - LLMs that can use tools and take actions, not just generate text
6. **All resources are free** - Andrew Ng, MIT, Jay Alammar, Hugging Face, Cohere, Berkeley, Arize
7. **One topic per day** - Depth over breadth; don't try to learn everything at once
8. **Build at every step** - Watching tutorials ≠ learning; you must build things

5. BPE — How It Actually Works (The Key Algorithm)
╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║                                                                  ║
║  ⚙️ BYTE PAIR ENCODING (BPE)                                    ║
║  The dominant sub-word tokenization algorithm                    ║
║                                                                  ║
║  Used by: GPT-2, GPT-3.5, GPT-4, GPT-4o, Llama 3               ║
║                                                                  ║
║  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────               ║
║                                                                  ║
║  KEY IDEA: Start with characters, iteratively merge              ║
║  the most FREQUENT pairs until vocab reaches target size         ║
║                                                                  ║
║  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────               ║
║                                                                  ║
║  STEP-BY-STEP EXAMPLE:                                           ║
║                                                                  ║
║  Training data: "hug hug hug bug bug pug"                        ║
║                                                                  ║
║  1️⃣ START: Split everything into characters                      ║
║     Vocab: [h, u, g, b, p, " "]                                 ║
║     Tokens: [h][u][g][ ][h][u][g][ ][h][u][g][ ][b][u][g]...   ║
║                                                                  ║
║  2️⃣ COUNT: Find most frequent pair                               ║
║     "u"+"g" appears 6 times (most frequent!)                    ║
║                                                                  ║
║  3️⃣ MERGE: Create new token "ug"                                 ║
║     Vocab: [h, u, g, b, p, " ", ug]                             ║
║     Tokens: [h][ug][ ][h][ug][ ][h][ug][ ][b][ug]...           ║
║                                                                  ║
║  4️⃣ REPEAT: Find next most frequent pair                         ║
║     "h"+"ug" appears 3 times → merge to "hug"                  ║
║     Vocab: [h, u, g, b, p, " ", ug, hug]                       ║
║                                                                  ║
║  5️⃣ KEEP GOING until vocab = target size (50K, 100K, 200K)      ║
║                                                                  ║
║  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────               ║
║                                                                  ║
║  🎯 Result: Common words = single token (efficient)              ║
║             Rare words = split into sub-tokens (still works)     ║
║             Vocabulary size = CONTROLLED (not random)            ║
║                                                                  ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

║   │ 0.5  │──w₁₂────▶│  MULTIPLY     │      │      │            ║
║   │ 0.3  │──w₁₃────▶│  & ADD        │─────▶│ 0.6  │            ║
║   │ 0.8  │──w₁₄────▶│  + bias       │      │      │            ║

║   │     (0.1)──w₁₁──╮                                   │       ║
║   │     (0.5)──w₁₂──┤                                   │       ║

🔦 BEAM SEARCH (Look Ahead, Keep Top-K Paths)
        ╔════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
        ║                                                        ║
        ║   🔦 BEAM SEARCH: Track Top-K Paths Simultaneously     ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ ║
        ║  │  ALGORITHM:                                       │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  Instead of 1 path (greedy), keep TOP-K paths!    │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  Example: K = 3 (beam width = 3)                  │ ║
        ║  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║                    Input: "How"                        ║
        ║                         │                              ║
        ║           ┌─────────────┼─────────────┐                ║
        ║           ▼             ▼             ▼                ║
        ║       ┌───────┐     ┌───────┐     ┌───────┐           ║
        ║       │ come  │     │  are  │     │  do   │           ║
        ║       │  24%  │     │  31%  │     │  26%  │           ║
        ║       └───────┘     └───────┘     └───────┘           ║
        ║           │             │             │                ║
        ║           │  Keep top 3 paths only! Discard rest      ║
        ║           │             │             │                ║
        ║           ▼             ▼             ▼                ║
        ║     "How come"     "How are"     "How do"              ║
        ║           │             │             │                ║
        ║           │             │             │                ║
        ║      [50K probs]   [50K probs]   [50K probs]           ║
        ║           │             │             │                ║
        ║  Calculate cumulative probabilities for ALL paths:     ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  • "How come animals": 24% × 36% = 8.64%               ║
        ║  • "How are you":      31% × 91% = 28.21% ← Best! ✅   ║
        ║  • "How do you":       26% × 63% = 16.38%              ║
        ║  • All other combos:   < 8%                            ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  🎯 Keep top 3 again, repeat until done                ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  ═══════════════════════════════════════════════       ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  ✅ BETTER than Greedy:                                ║
        ║     • Explores multiple paths                          ║
        ║     • Sometimes finds better sequences                 ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  ❌ STILL HAS ISSUES:                                  ║
        ║     • Can still repeat                                 ║
        ║     • More complex/slower                              ║
        ║     • K is fixed (not adaptive)                        ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  ❌ NOT USED IN MODERN LLMs!                           ║
        ║                                                        ║

🎲 TOP-K SAMPLING (Fixed K, Random Sampling)
        ╔════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
        ║                                                        ║
        ║       🎲 TOP-K SAMPLING: Sample from Top-K Tokens      ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ ║
        ║  │  ALGORITHM:                                       │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  1. Get probabilities from LLM                    │ ║
        ║  │  2. Keep ONLY top-K highest probability tokens    │ ║
        ║  │  3. DISCARD everything else                       │ ║
        ║  │  4. RANDOMLY sample from top-K (weighted)         │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  🎲 THIS IS WHERE RANDOMNESS COMES IN!            │ ║
        ║  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ ║
        ║  │  EXAMPLE: K = 3                                   │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  Input: "How"                                     │ ║
        ║  │  Probabilities:                                   │ ║
        ║  │    • r:    37%  ← Keep (Top 3)                    │ ║
        ║  │    • is:   21%  ← Keep (Top 3)                    │ ║
        ║  │    • to:   15%  ← Keep (Top 3)                    │ ║
        ║  │    ─────────────────────────────                  │ ║
        ║  │    • hard:  8%  ← DISCARD                         │ ║
        ║  │    • when:  5%  ← DISCARD                         │ ║
        ║  │    • xyz:   3%  ← DISCARD (grammatically wrong)   │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  Now randomly sample from {r, is, to}             │ ║
        ║  │  according to their probabilities                 │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  Run 1: Picks "r"    (37% chance)                 │ ║
        ║  │  Run 2: Picks "is"   (21% chance)                 │ ║
        ║  │  Run 3: Picks "r"    (37% chance)                 │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  ✅ Different outputs each time!                  │ ║
        ║  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ ║
        ║  │  ❌ PROBLEM: K is FIXED!                          │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  Scenario A: Model is VERY confident              │ ║
        ║  │  ┌────────────────────────────┐                   │ ║
        ║  │  │ lot:   89%  ← Very high!   │                   │ ║
        ║  │  │ much:   3%  ← Very low     │                   │ ║
        ║  │  │ high:   2%  ← Very low     │                   │ ║
        ║  │  └────────────────────────────┘                   │ ║
        ║  │  Top-K (K=3) still keeps 3 tokens                 │ ║
        ║  │  even though "much" and "high" are unlikely!      │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  Scenario B: Model is UNCERTAIN                   │ ║
        ║  │  ┌────────────────────────────┐                   │ ║
        ║  │  │ r:     31%                 │                   │ ║
        ║  │  │ is:    29%                 │                   │ ║
        ║  │  │ to:    12%                 │                   │ ║
        ║  │  │ do:    10%  ← Also good!   │                   │ ║
        ║  │  │ can:    8%  ← Also good!   │                   │ ║
        ║  │  └────────────────────────────┘                   │ ║
        ║  │  Top-K (K=3) only keeps 3 tokens                  │ ║
        ║  │  Missing "do" and "can" which are reasonable!     │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  💡 K should be DYNAMIC, not fixed!               │ ║

⭐ TOP-P SAMPLING (BEST - Used in Modern LLMs!)
        ╔════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  ⭐ TOP-P SAMPLING (Nucleus Sampling): ADAPTIVE K!     ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ ║
        ║  │  ALGORITHM:                                       │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  1. Get probabilities from LLM                    │ ║
        ║  │  2. Sort tokens by probability (highest first)    │ ║
        ║  │  3. Keep adding tokens until cumulative           │ ║
        ║  │     probability EXCEEDS threshold P               │ ║
        ║  │  4. DISCARD rest                                  │ ║
        ║  │  5. RANDOMLY sample from selected tokens          │ ║
        ║  │                                                   │ ║
        ║  │  🎯 K is DYNAMIC! Changes based on distribution!  │ ║
        ║  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════  ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  EXAMPLE 1: Model is CONFIDENT (P = 88%)               ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  Input: "Thanks a"                                     ║
        ║  Probabilities:                                        ║
        ║  ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐              ║
        ║  │ lot:   89%  ← EXCEEDS 88%! ✅        │              ║
        ║  │ much:   3%                           │              ║
        ║  │ high:   2%                           │              ║
        ║  │ ... (discard all)                    │              ║
        ║  └─────────────────────────────────────┘              ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  Cumulative: 89% > 88% threshold                       ║
        ║  ✅ Only consider "lot" (K = 1 dynamically!)           ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════  ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  EXAMPLE 2: Model is UNCERTAIN (P = 88%)               ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  Input: "How"                                          ║
        ║  Probabilities:                                        ║
        ║  ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐              ║
        ║  │ r:     31%  ← Cum:  31%              │              ║
        ║  │ is:    29%  ← Cum:  60%              │              ║
        ║  │ to:    12%  ← Cum:  72%              │              ║
        ║  │ do:    16%  ← Cum:  88%  STOP! ✅    │              ║
        ║  │ ───────────────────────────────────  │              ║
        ║  │ can:    8%  ← DISCARD (exceeds 88%)  │              ║
        ║  │ ... (discard rest)                   │              ║
        ║  └─────────────────────────────────────┘              ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  Cumulative: 88% = 88% threshold                       ║
        ║  ✅ Consider {r, is, to, do} (K = 4 dynamically!)      ║

        ║                                                        ║
        ║  🎯 KEY ADVANTAGE:                                     ║
        ║                                                        ║
        ║  • Confident model → Small K (focused)                 ║

🎯 Core Concepts Summary
1. **Base models are powerful but not useful** - They continue text brilliantly but don't answer questions
2. **Post-training has 2 stages** - SFT (instruction following) + RL (safety & alignment)
3. **SFT = Teaching by example** - Show model thousands of prompt-response pairs
4. **Training is identical to pre-training** - Same algorithm, just swap the data
5. **Data quality > Data quantity** - 14K expert examples beats billions of random internet text
6. **Bigger models = Better continuations** - GPT-2 (basic) vs Llama 3.1 405B (coherent)
7. **SFT models still need RL** - Not ready for deployment after SFT alone

🔧 DIAGRAM 7: SFT Training Process
╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║            🎓 SFT TRAINING = IDENTICAL TO PRE-TRAINING!                   ║
║               (Just swap the data, everything else same)                  ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  STEP 1: Load Base Model                                                 │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
  │  🔮 BASE MODEL                   │
  │  (After pre-training)            │
  │                                  │
  │  • Has 405B parameters           │
  │  • Knows language patterns       │
  │  • Has world knowledge           │
  │  • Predicts next token           │
  │  📛 Doesn't answer questions     │
  └─────────────────────────────────┘


┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  STEP 2: Replace Training Data                                           │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  BEFORE (Pre-training):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Random internet text:                                    │
  │  "The cat sat on the mat. It was a sunny day..."          │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  AFTER (SFT):
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Instruction-response pairs:                              │
  │  "<PROMPT> What is the capital of France? </PROMPT>       │
  │   <RESPONSE> The capital of France is Paris. </RESPONSE>" │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘


┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  STEP 3: Run Training (IDENTICAL algorithm!)                             │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  FOR each training iteration:

    1️⃣  Sample from SFT data
        → Get: "What is the capital of France?"

    2️⃣  Forward pass (predict next tokens)
        → Model tries to predict: "The capital of France is Paris"

    3️⃣  Calculate loss (same cross-entropy loss!)
        → Compare prediction vs actual response

    4️⃣  Backward pass (compute gradients)
        → Calculate how to adjust weights

    5️⃣  Update weights (same optimizer - Adam!)
        → Adjust model to better answer this type of question

    6️⃣  Repeat for thousands of examples


┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  STEP 4: Get SFT Model                                                   │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
  │  ✅ SFT MODEL                    │
  │  (After SFT training)            │
  │                                  │
  │  • Same 405B parameters          │
  │  • Knows language patterns       │
  │  • Has world knowledge           │
  │  • Predicts next token           │
  │  ✅ ANSWERS QUESTIONS!           │
  │  📛 Not safe yet (needs RL)      │
  └─────────────────────────────────┘

│  🔑 CRITICAL INSIGHT                                                     │
│                                                                          │
│  You DON'T change:                                                       │
│  • Loss function (still cross-entropy)                                  │
│  • Optimizer (still Adam/AdamW)                                         │
│  • Training algorithm (still next-token prediction)                     │
│  • Model architecture (same transformer)                                │
│                                                                          │
│  You ONLY change:                                                        │
│  • Training data (internet → instruction pairs)                         │
│                                                                          │

Interview Prep

Explain It All in 2 Minutes

Read this out loud. If you can say it naturally, you own every concept.

So you want to know how ChatGPT works? Here's the whole story:

First, they vacuumed up the entire internet — 2.7 billion web pages — and cleaned it. Then they built a translator called a tokenizer that converts words into numbers, because computers can't read text.

Those numbers go through 4 stages: the tokenizer chops text into IDs, an embedding layer turns IDs into rich vectors, transformer blocks process those vectors through dozens of smart filters, and a softmax function picks the most likely next word out of 50,000 options.

To teach it, they run a loop a million times: guess the next word, check how wrong it was, adjust billions of tiny knobs, repeat. This costs $100 million and takes months on thousands of GPUs.

But a trained model only gives you probabilities — it doesn't actually write text yet. Text generation is a for loop: pick one token using Top-P sampling (keep the most likely words until they add up to 90%, randomly pick from those), add it to the sentence, repeat. Temperature controls creativity — low for code, high for stories.

After all that, the model is a genius who can't hold a conversation. So they show it ~13,000 perfect Q&A examples (that's SFT — supervised fine-tuning), and it learns to answer questions.

But it's still not great — answers are correct but lazy and sometimes unsafe. So they run reinforcement learning: let the model practice by generating multiple responses, score them (automatically for math/code, or using a reward model trained on human rankings for everything else), and PPO reinforces the high-scoring ones. The result? Detailed, helpful, safe, polite responses.

But how do you know if the model is any good? Evaluation comes in two flavors: offline (before deployment) and online (after). Offline uses benchmarks like PIQA for common sense and HumanEval for coding — standardized tests with right answers. Online uses real users: thumbs up/down in ChatGPT, or LM Arena where UC Berkeley researchers (LMSYS group) built a blind taste test — two anonymous models respond, you vote, identities revealed, ELO rankings updated. 2.9 million votes across 211+ models.

And the model itself? It's just one piece. The full system has safety filters checking both input AND output, a prompt enhancer fixing your typos and grammar, the response generator using Top-P sampling, and session management that appends your entire chat history to every new prompt — that's how it 'remembers' your conversation.

And that's it — that's ChatGPT, Claude, Llama. Same recipe: vacuum internet → clean → tokenize → train → generate → SFT → RL → evaluate → wrap in safety + session management. Some just use bigger LEGO towers than others.

Q1 4-Step LLM Learning Path
What are the 4 steps in the AI learning path, in order?
The 4 steps are: (1) ML Fundamentals, (2) Transformers, (3) Pre-training/Fine-tuning/RAG, (4) AI Apps & Agents. Order matters — skip a step and the house falls down.
Q2 How LLMs Are Built + Data Prep
What is Common Crawl?
Common Crawl is a free public dataset that crawls 2.7 billion web pages every 1-2 months. Hugging Face's FineWeb cleans it down to 44 TB / 15 trillion tokens.
Q3 Tokenization
What does BPE stand for?
BPE = Byte Pair Encoding. It starts with individual characters, finds the most frequent pair, merges them into one token, and repeats. This gives a vocabulary of 50K-200K sub-word pieces.
Q4 Transformers
What did the 2017 paper 'Attention Is All You Need' replace?
The Transformer replaced RNNs, which read text one word at a time (slow and forgetful). Transformers read ALL words at once using attention — each word can 'look at' every other word.
Q5 Model Architecture
What are the 4 stages of the LLM pipeline (text in → next token out)?
Text flows through 4 stages: Tokenizer (text→IDs) → Embedding (IDs→vectors) → Transformer Blocks (process vectors) → Linear+Softmax (pick next token from 50K options).
Q6 Model Training
What is the training loop (in order)?
The training loop is FLBO: Forward pass (guess next word) → Loss (grade the guess) → Backward pass (calculate how to fix each weight) → Optimizer (actually adjust billions of weights). Repeat 1M+ times.
Q7 Text Generation
What sampling method do modern LLMs (ChatGPT, Claude) actually use for text generation?
Top-P (Nucleus) Sampling is used in practice. It keeps the most likely tokens until their cumulative probability hits a threshold (e.g., 90%), then randomly picks from those. The K is dynamic — that's the key advantage over Top-K.
Q8 Post Training SFT
What does SFT (Supervised Fine-Tuning) use as training data?
SFT uses 14K-100K expert-curated Q&A pairs (e.g., InstructGPT ~13K, Alpaca 52K). The training math is identical to pre-training — only the DATA changes from messy internet to perfect conversations.
Q9 Reinforcement Learning
What is PPO's job in Reinforcement Learning?
PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) updates the model's parameters to reinforce good responses. The reward model scores responses; PPO uses those scores to adjust weights so the model produces more high-scoring answers.
Q10 Evaluation of LLMs
What is LM Arena (lmarena.ai)?
LM Arena is a crowdsourcing platform by UC Berkeley researchers (LMSYS group). Two anonymous models respond to the same prompt, users vote for the better one, then identities are revealed. 2.9M+ votes across 211+ models, with ELO rankings.
